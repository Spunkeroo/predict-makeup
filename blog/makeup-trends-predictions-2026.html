Here is what is trending, what is fading, and what the prediction markets say about the future of makeup.

AI-Powered Beauty Technology

Artificial intelligence is no longer a novelty in beauty — it is becoming the foundation of how consumers discover, try, and purchase makeup. The AI beauty tech market reached $4.2 billion in 2025 and is accelerating as every major brand integrates AI into their customer experience.

Virtual Try-On Goes Mainstream

AI-powered virtual try-on technology has matured dramatically. Platforms like Perfect Corp's YouCam and L'Oreal's Modiface now render realistic makeup on live video with accurate color matching, proper light interaction, and natural blending that is nearly indistinguishable from actual product application. The technology works across skin tones, face shapes, and lighting conditions — a far cry from the obviously fake overlays of early AR beauty tools.

The impact on purchasing behavior is measurable. Brands report that products with virtual try-on experience see 2-3x higher conversion rates and 30% fewer returns compared to products without try-on. For consumers, it eliminates the biggest frustration in online beauty shopping: buying a shade that looks nothing like it did on screen.

In 2026, virtual try-on is expanding beyond lipstick and foundation to complex looks: full eye makeup, contour and highlight, blush placement, and complete curated looks. Some platforms now let you upload a photo of a celebrity or influencer look and see it virtually applied to your own face, adjusted for your features and skin tone.

AI Shade Matching

Finding the right foundation shade has been the beauty industry's most persistent problem. AI is solving it. Companies like Revieve, bareMinerals (with its MADE-2-FIT custom foundation), and il Makiage use AI algorithms that analyze skin tone from selfies under various lighting conditions to recommend precise shade matches.

The technology accounts for undertone (warm, cool, neutral), surface color, and even seasonal skin tone variations. Il Makiage's AI shade matching, which uses a quiz combined with image analysis, claims a 90% first-match success rate — compared to the estimated 50% success rate of in-store shade matching by sales associates.

Custom-blended foundation is the next frontier. Several brands now offer AI-analyzed, custom-mixed foundation that is blended to your exact skin tone and shipped to your door. The cost premium over off-the-shelf foundation is shrinking as the technology scales, and the perfect-match experience creates exceptional customer loyalty.

AI-Powered Skin Analysis

Smartphone apps can now perform skin analysis that rivals a dermatologist's initial assessment. AI analyzes photos for: pore size and distribution, wrinkle depth and pattern, pigmentation irregularities, acne type and severity, dark circles, redness patterns, and overall skin health scores. This analysis generates personalized skincare and makeup recommendations based on your actual skin condition, not a generic quiz.

The most advanced systems track skin changes over time, correlating improvements or deterioration with specific products, weather conditions, diet, sleep patterns, and stress levels. This longitudinal data is creating a new paradigm of evidence-based personal beauty care, where product recommendations are based on what actually works for your skin rather than marketing claims.

The Clean Makeup Revolution

Clean beauty has evolved from a niche category to a mainstream expectation. In 2026, "clean" is no longer a differentiator — it is the baseline. Consumers, especially Gen Z and Millennial buyers who drive the majority of beauty spending growth, expect transparency about ingredients and will not tolerate formulations that include substances they consider harmful.

What "Clean" Means in 2026

The definition of clean beauty has become more standardized. Sephora's "Clean + Planet Positive" program, Credo Beauty's "Clean Standard," and the EU's cosmetics regulations have created a broadly accepted framework. In 2026, "clean" generally means:

  • Free from: parabens, phthalates, formaldehyde and formaldehyde-releasing agents, mineral oil, coal tar dyes, oxybenzone, triclosan, and sulfates
  • Transparent labeling: full ingredient lists with explanations of what each ingredient does, sourced in readable language, not just INCI names
  • Third-party verified: certifications from EWG, COSMOS, or Leaping Bunny rather than self-declared "clean" claims
  • Sustainably sourced: ingredients traced back to responsible suppliers, with documentation available to consumers

The regulatory environment is tightening. The EU has banned over 1,600 cosmetic ingredients compared to approximately 11 in the US, but American regulation is catching up. Several states have passed cosmetics safety laws, and federal legislation is progressing. Brands that proactively adopt the highest global standards are positioning themselves for regulatory compliance rather than scrambling to reformulate later.

Performance Catches Up

The biggest criticism of clean makeup has been performance: clean products did not last as long, were not as pigmented, and did not feel as luxurious as their conventional counterparts. That gap has largely closed in 2026. Advances in plant-derived pigments, bio-fermented actives, and innovative formulation technology have produced clean products that perform as well as — and in some cases better than — conventional formulas.

Brands like Kosas, Ilia, Saie, Tower 28, and Rare Beauty have proven that clean ingredients and high performance are not mutually exclusive. Their products consistently receive top marks in both consumer testing and professional reviews. The era of choosing between clean and effective is over.

K-Beauty Innovations in 2026

Korean beauty continues to set global trends, staying 2-3 years ahead of Western markets in product innovation, ingredient research, and beauty philosophy. The K-beauty trends of 2026 reflect a maturing market that is moving beyond novelty toward sophisticated, science-backed approaches to beauty.

Skin Barrier Obsession

The Korean skincare focus on barrier health — maintaining the skin's protective lipid layer — has become the dominant philosophy in global beauty. Products featuring ceramides, centella asiatica, panthenol, and niacinamide are formulated specifically to strengthen and repair the skin barrier rather than strip it with harsh actives.

This philosophy extends to makeup. Korean makeup brands are formulating color cosmetics with skincare-grade barrier-supporting ingredients. Foundations with ceramides, lip products with hyaluronic acid, and mascaras with peptides reflect the K-beauty principle that makeup should improve your skin while you wear it, not just cover it.

Glass Skin 2.0

The "glass skin" look — impossibly dewy, translucent, poreless-looking skin — remains the aspiration, but the technique has evolved. Glass skin 2.0 in 2026 focuses on internal glow rather than surface shine. The approach layers lightweight hydrating products (essences, serums, moisturizers) to create genuine skin luminosity, then uses minimal, sheer-coverage makeup to enhance rather than mask.

Key products in the glass skin routine include: fermented essences (like those from SK-II and Missha), gel-cream hybrid moisturizers, water-drop primers that create a luminous base, and barely-there tinted moisturizers or skin tints that allow the prepared skin to show through.

Cushion Compact Evolution

Cushion compacts, the K-beauty invention that disrupted foundation application globally, continue to evolve. The 2026 generation features multi-layered sponges that deliver different formulas (primer, foundation, SPF) in a single application. Smart cushions with built-in UV sensors remind you when to reapply SPF. And the formula range has expanded from light-coverage BB creams to full-coverage foundations, concealers, and even contour cushions.

Hot Trend

"Jelly" Textures

K-beauty's latest texture innovation is jelly-format makeup: bouncy, translucent products that feel like skincare but deliver color. Jelly blush, jelly highlighter, and jelly lip tints apply seamlessly with fingers, blend without effort, and give a natural, dewy finish that powder products cannot replicate. Brands like rom&nd, Peripera, and Laneige are leading the category.

The Skin-First Makeup Philosophy

The "skinimalism" movement that began during the pandemic has matured into a permanent shift in how consumers approach makeup. The philosophy is simple: invest in skincare so you need less makeup, then use makeup to enhance rather than conceal.

This shows up in product trends:

  • Skin tints over foundation: Sheer, hydrating skin tints and tinted moisturizers outsell full-coverage foundations for the first time. Consumers want their actual skin to show through, just perfected and evened out.
  • Multi-use products: Products that serve double duty (lip and cheek tints, cream blush that works as eyeshadow, SPF-infused tinted moisturizer) reduce the number of products in the routine while increasing convenience.
  • Skincare-makeup hybrids: Every major brand now offers products that blur the line between skincare and makeup. Foundations with SPF, niacinamide, and hyaluronic acid. Lip products with peptides and ceramides. Setting sprays with antioxidants.
  • Prep over cover: The focus has shifted from covering imperfections to preparing skin so imperfections are minimized before makeup is applied. Primers with blurring properties, pore-minimizing treatments, and color-correcting skincare reduce the need for heavy makeup layers.

Trending Looks for 2026

Hot Trend

Latte Makeup (Warm Neutrals)

The "latte makeup" trend that exploded on TikTok has evolved into a sophisticated warm-neutral aesthetic that works across all skin tones. Think: warm brown eyeshadow, caramel-toned blush, nude-pink lips, and golden highlighter. The look is polished but wearable, suitable for everything from office days to date nights. The key is keeping everything in the same warm tonal family for a cohesive, monochromatic effect.

Hot Trend

Glazed Donut Skin

Hailey Bieber's "glazed donut" skin remains the aspirational finish in 2026, though the technique has been refined and democratized. The look is achieved through layered hydration, a luminous primer, minimal base product, and strategic highlighter placement. The focus is on a healthy, hydrated glow from within rather than a shimmery surface sheen. Products like Saie's Glowy Super Gel and Rhode's Peptide Glazing Fluid have become category-defining products.

Rising

Cherry Makeup

Deep cherry and burgundy tones are the statement color of 2026. Cherry red lips, berry-stained cheeks, and wine-toned eyeshadow create a rich, romantic look that contrasts with the neutral dominance of recent years. The trend is particularly strong in evening and editorial makeup, offering a modern take on classic glamour.

Rising

Soft Glam Revival

The maximalist glam that defined the Instagram era (heavy contour, dramatic eyes, overlined lips) is making a comeback in softer form. Think of it as "glam lite" — the skills and techniques of full glam applied with a lighter hand. Blended contour that enhances bone structure without obvious stripes, eyeshadow that has depth without being dramatic, and lips that are defined without being overlined.

At Peak

Blush Everywhere

The oversized blush trend — applying blush not just on the apples of cheeks but across the nose, temples, and forehead — is at peak saturation. The "sunburnt" and "I just came in from the cold" effects created by expansive blush application give an energetic, youthful flush that photographs beautifully. Cream and liquid blushes from Rare Beauty, Patrick Ta, and Danessa Myricks dominate the category.

Rising

Graphic Liner

Graphic eyeliner is back with a new twist: instead of the sharp, symmetrical wings of past eras, 2026's graphic liner is artistic and experimental. Floating liner (applied above the crease rather than at the lash line), geometric shapes, negative-space designs, and colorful accents reflect a playful, creative approach to eye makeup. TikTok and Instagram tutorials have made these advanced techniques accessible to everyday consumers.

Ingredient Trends to Watch

The ingredients driving makeup innovation in 2026:

  • Bakuchiol: The plant-based retinol alternative has crossed from skincare into makeup. Foundations and primers featuring bakuchiol claim anti-aging benefits during wear. Unlike retinol, bakuchiol does not cause sun sensitivity, making it ideal for daytime products.
  • Squalane: This lightweight, non-comedogenic oil is replacing silicones as the primary emollient in premium foundations and concealers. Derived from olives or sugarcane, squalane provides a smooth, dewy finish without the synthetic feel of dimethicone.
  • Adaptogens: Ashwagandha, reishi mushroom, and turmeric are appearing in makeup formulations marketed as "stress-reducing" or "cortisol-conscious." The science is preliminary, but the consumer demand for products that address the skin effects of stress is real.
  • Peptides in color cosmetics: Peptides (short chains of amino acids) are crossing from serums into lipsticks, mascaras, and brow products. The claim: these products treat while they color, stimulating collagen production in lips and promoting lash and brow growth.
  • Bio-fermented pigments: Fermentation-derived color pigments offer vibrant, long-lasting color from natural sources. These bio-pigments are replacing synthetic dyes in clean beauty formulations without sacrificing color payoff.
  • Probiotic makeup: Following the success of probiotic skincare, makeup products containing live cultures or prebiotic fibers claim to support the skin's microbiome during wear. Primers and foundations with microbiome-friendly formulations are gaining shelf space.

Sustainability and Refillable Beauty

Sustainability in beauty has moved from optional to expected. The environmental impact of the beauty industry — an estimated 120 billion units of packaging per year, most of it not recycled — is driving systemic change in how products are packaged, sold, and disposed of.

Refillable Packaging

Refillable makeup is the biggest sustainability trend in 2026. Luxury brands led the way (Guerlain, Dior, Kjaer Weis), and now mass-market brands are following. MAC's Back to MAC program, Fenty Beauty's refillable lipstick, and Kosas' refill system show that refillable packaging works at every price point. The economics are straightforward: refills cost 30-40% less than buying a new product, and the premium packaging becomes a long-term investment rather than single-use waste.

Waterless Beauty

Waterless (anhydrous) formulations are gaining traction as both a sustainability measure and a performance advantage. Water is the #1 ingredient in most liquid makeup, but removing it creates more concentrated, potent products that last longer, require less preservative, and ship lighter (reducing carbon footprint). Solid foundations, powder-to-liquid products, and concentrated balms represent the waterless future.

Biodegradable Glitter

Traditional cosmetic glitter is microplastic that persists in the environment for centuries. The EU's microplastic ban, which affects cosmetic glitter, has accelerated the development of biodegradable alternatives made from plant cellulose, mica, and other compostable materials. These alternatives now match the sparkle and color range of conventional glitter, eliminating the last excuse for using plastic-based shimmer.

Inclusive Beauty Expansion

The inclusive beauty movement that Fenty Beauty catalyzed in 2017 with 40 foundation shades has become an industry standard. In 2026, the bar has been raised significantly:

Shade ranges: A minimum of 40 shades is now expected for any foundation launch from a major brand. Brands like Fenty (50 shades), MAC (60+ shades), and Il Makiage (50 shades) continue to push the range wider, with particular attention to deep and very deep shades that were historically underserved.

Undertone matching: Beyond just light-to-dark shade ranges, brands are offering more precise undertone options. Instead of 3 undertones (warm, cool, neutral), leading brands now offer 5-7 undertone categories, including olive, golden, and red undertones that were previously ignored.

Gender-inclusive marketing: Beauty brands are increasingly marketing to all genders. Brands like Fluide, Jecca Blac, and Non Gender Specific were early movers, but now mainstream brands have dropped gendered language from their marketing and product design.

Adaptive packaging: Beauty products designed for consumers with visual impairments, limited dexterity, or other disabilities are expanding. Braille labels, magnetic closures, easy-grip packaging, and audio descriptions of shades and products make beauty more accessible.

Social Media and Beauty Culture

TikTok remains the dominant platform for beauty trend creation and product discovery in 2026. The platform's algorithm-driven content distribution means a single viral video can create overnight demand that empties shelves. Beauty brands have adapted their entire product development and marketing cycles around TikTok virality.

Key Dynamics

"Get Ready With Me" (GRWM) content is the dominant beauty content format, generating billions of views monthly. The format is effective because it shows products in use on real people in real lighting, providing more authentic product demonstrations than traditional advertising.

Dupe culture continues to thrive. TikTok creators who find affordable alternatives to luxury products drive massive sales for drugstore brands. e.l.f., NYX, and Essence are the biggest beneficiaries, with products going viral as dupes for Charlotte Tilbury, Tom Ford, and Dior items at a fraction of the price.

Deinfluencing has matured from a backlash trend into a legitimate content category. Creators who honestly review products and tell followers what NOT to buy have built large, trusting audiences. This transparency is pushing brands to ensure their products actually deliver on claims, because a viral deinfluencing video can tank sales overnight.

AI-generated beauty content is emerging as a concern. Deepfake filters and AI-enhanced selfies create unrealistic beauty standards that are indistinguishable from real images. The industry and platforms are grappling with how to maintain authenticity when the technology to fake flawless skin is freely available to anyone with a smartphone.

Our Top 10 Beauty Predictions for 2026

  1. AI shade matching will become more accurate than in-store matching. AI algorithms analyzing selfies will recommend better foundation matches than in-person consultation at beauty counters. The data advantage is insurmountable.
  2. Refillable packaging will reach 20% of prestige beauty sales. Consumer demand, brand economics (refills are higher margin), and regulatory pressure will drive rapid adoption.
  3. K-beauty "jelly" textures will dominate the cream product category. The bouncy, translucent jelly format will expand from blush and highlighter to foundation, concealer, and bronzer.
  4. At least one major brand will launch AI-custom-blended products at mass market prices. Custom foundation at $25 rather than $75 will democratize personalized beauty.
  5. Skincare-makeup hybrid products will outsell pure makeup in the face category. Products that treat skin while providing coverage will become the default rather than the exception.
  6. The warm neutral "latte" palette will be challenged by a cool-toned revival. After years of warm dominance, cool mauves, taupes, and silvers will emerge as the countertrend in late 2026.
  7. Probiotic makeup will move from novelty to evidence-based. Published research demonstrating microbiome benefits of specific makeup formulations will validate the category.
  8. TikTok Shop will become the #1 channel for beauty product discovery. In-app purchasing driven by content creator demonstrations will overtake Sephora.com and Ulta.com for new product discovery (not total sales, but first-discovery moments).
  9. Waterless formulations will gain 10%+ market share in base products. Solid and powder-based foundations and concealers that activate with water or serum will gain mainstream acceptance.
  10. Biodegradable glitter will fully replace plastic glitter in prestige brands. Regulatory pressure (EU microplastic ban) and consumer demand will make plastic glitter unacceptable for any brand with sustainability claims.
